Contribution

Our contributions are novel, as we are the first team to introduce Emiliania huxleyi parts to the iGEM Parts Registry. We believe it is an important step in developing synthetic biology tools for this organism. By contributing these parts, we hope to lay the groundwork for future teams into E. huxleyi, allowing other teams to further explore and experiment with this underdeveloped organism.

We are just beginning to explore E. huxleyi and its biological machinery, particularly its calcification pathway, which is still not well understood. The expression system we've developed is a starting point for downstream analysis, including functional studies of calmodulin, which plays an essential role in calcium-dependent processes such as calcification.

Our designs for genomic editing tools also reflect work that could guide future research and the direction of our team, given we would like to continue this beyond the iGEM cycle. This conceptual framework offers opportunities for further development, enabling other teams to build upon our approach. Through these contributions, we hope to inspire and support the iGEM community in pushing the boundaries of synthetic biology.

Expression construct

We aim to develop an expression construct for studying our organism of interest i.e Emiliania Huxleyi by incorporating the calmodulin gene into a plasmid vector under the control of a strong promoter, to induce and express heterologous recombinant protein derived from E. huxley in E. coli for downstream analysis. This system is essential for isolating from E. coli and studying calmodulin as a heterologous recombinant protein derived from Emiliania huxleyi.

We have designed part (BBa_K5365001) for characterization, which encodes the protein calmodulin (CAM) from Emiliania huxleyi. This protein plays a crucial role in the unique calcification pathway of E. huxleyi. To facilitate its expression in E. coli, we have codon optimized the gene and constructed an expression vector, (BBa_K5365777), which contains the CAM gene along with designed pET-28a (BBa_K5365003). This construct includes a His-tag at the C-terminus of the CAM protein to simplify purification. The standard pET-28a plasmids were either incompatible with RFC[10] assembly compatibility or had been modified which did not accommodate our requirements.

E. huxleyi homologous recombination constructs

We aim to develop genome editing tools for Emiliania huxleyi by leveraging CRISPR-Cas9, homologous recombination, and homology-directed repair (HDR). The cell's homologous recombination repair pathway is activated after CRISPR-Cas9 introduces double-stranded breaks at specific target sites in the E. huxleyi genome. This process uses donor DNA containing left and right homology arms, which are designed to match the sequences flanking the break. These arms align the donor DNA with the broken genomic site, facilitating the insertion of desired sequences like the T7 RNA polymerase, T7 promoter, Lac operator, and CAM sequence. This precise integration through HDR allows controlled overexpression of CAM using the lac operon and selectable markers.

We have chosen to work with CAM to explore its role in Emiliania huxleyi and to study its overexpression using homologous recombination techniques. To achieve this, we designed a pUC19 vector(BBa_K5365002) compatible with the RFC10 standard, since earlier parts were not assembly-compatible. This vector will serve as a general-purpose tool for introducing genes or genome editing tools into E. huxleyi.

Specifically, we created (BBa_K5365004) and (BBa_K5365005) as the left and right homology arms, respectively, to facilitate the insertion of (BBa_K3633015) from the iGEM Registry, which codes for a T7 promoter, along with(BBa_K3842012) which includes the lac operator. This is done using a plasmid construct (BBa_K5365009). The system will work in tandem with (BBa_K5365010) to ensure full functionality.


Following, we designed (BBa_K5365006) and (BBa_K5365007), coding right and left homology regions respectively, targeting the E. huxleyi RuBisCO (rbcl) gene: the right homology arm corresponds to the rbCL coding sequence, while the left homology arm covers the downstream region, respectively. Importantly, the rbcl promoter, located upstream of the rbcl protein, will drive the transcription of the T7 RNA polymerase (K1706007) and the lacI repressor (K1088018), which we are inserting before the rbcl gene in the E. huxleyi genome. To achieve this, we have designed a plasmid construct (BBa_K5365010). These homology regions and promoter elements will ensure efficient expression of the T7 system, allowing for the controlled overexpression of CAM in E. huxleyi.

Software Contribution

We lay down a novel framework for the wider synthetic biology community who are interested in characterizing the impact of a particular gene in understudied species or in low-resource settings. Our framework leverages the power of transcriptomics data deposited publicly to generate testable hypotheses with multiple lines of evidence. Our computational package ‘genecorder’ enables rapid re-analysis of public RNA-sequencing datasets and can be leveraged to probe the regulatory mechanisms of a gene

In particular, GENECorder enables the following:

  • Automated Data Fetching: Seamlessly integrates with the NCBI SRA toolkit to retrieve transcriptomic data for any available organism listed on Ensembl, including single-ended and paired-ended bulk RNA-seq data.
  • Streamlined Data Analysis: Utilizes bioinformatics tools such as Kallisto, kb-python, and PyTXimport for processing, quantification, and normalization of gene expression data.
  • User-Friendly Design: Designed with a simple command-line interface that allows users with no prior bioinformatics experience to easily run comprehensive RNA-seq analysis.
  • Visual and Statistical Insights: Offers built-in functions for plotting gene expression data, creating co-expression heatmaps, and performing differential gene expression analysis using DESeq2.

While designing genecorder, we focused on desgining the software for a user who might not necessarily be familiar with coding/programming. Our tool is organized as modular set of subcommands. We provide extensive documentation and examples demonstrating how each subcommand of the tool can be used. All the subcommands can be invoked via the command line and are mostly independent of each other thereby allowing the user to start from any of the steps of the workflow (FIgure 1) We envision genecorder will be used by the broader synthetic biology and iGEM communities for carrying out rapid re-analysis and target discovery of mechanisms underlying regulation of gene(s) of interest.
