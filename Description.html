Problem: Climate Crisis & Rising CO2 emissions

The Climate Crisis is currently one of the most pressing global issues, driven by excessive emissions of greenhouse gases—especially carbon dioxide—as a result of uncontrolled human activities. This has led to rising global temperatures and increasingly frequent extreme weather events.

The rising levels of CO2 in the atmosphere demand our immediate attention. According to the NOAA Global Monitoring Lab, carbon dioxide accounted for about two-thirds of the total warming impact from all human-generated greenhouse gases in 2021.

Existing Solutions Using CCUS Technology

One way to tackle this is through Carbon Capture, Utilization, and Storage (CCUS) technologies, which capture CO2 emissions from power plants or industrial facilities. The captured CO2 is then compressed and transported for various applications or injected underground into deep geological formations for long-term storage.

However, current technologies of CCUS also have their limitations in terms of environmental impact of storing CO2 and high costs of implementation. It is also limited by the availability of geological space to store the huge amounts of CO2 that actually needs to be removed to bring global warming in check.

Algae-based Carbon Capture Approach

Thus our project presents an alternative solution harnessing the potential of algae-based carbon capture as they have remarkable abilities to absorb carbon dioxide (CO2) at significantly higher rates than traditional plants and their versatility allows them to thrive in challenging environments, such as wastewater, oceans, and saltwater lakes, making them ideal candidates for large-scale implementation without competing for valuable land or freshwater resources.

The unique capability of certain algal species to rapidly convert CO2 into biomass, including the formation of stable inorganic carbonates, allows them to effectively lock away carbon for millennia. This presents a more resilient solution as carbon is stored in forms that cannot be easily released back into the atmosphere. Additionally, algal cultivation also utilises nutrients from agricultural runoff or wastewater, helping to reduce nutrient pollution while capturing carbon.

Along with this, the biomass generated through algal carbon capture can be repurposed for biofuels, animal feed, and biodegradable plastics, promoting a circular economy and adding economic incentives for industries to integrate these systems to reduce their carbon footprints.

Traditional CCUS technologies can require 1-2 GJ per ton of CO2 captured which translates to an energy cost of approximately $30-$60 per ton of CO2 captured. Algal based carbon capture generally has lower energy requirements, estimated at about 1-2 GJ per ton of CO2 captured, translating to an energy cost of about $10-$20 per ton of CO2 captured.

However, algal based solutions mainly focus on sequestering carbon using photosynthesis which leads to biomass production. Carbon dioxide captured in this manner can be easily released via degradation. Thus we need a way to permanently trap CO2 and ensure it is released back into the environment.

Our Innovative Approach

Thats why our team is working on the marine coccolithophore E. huxleyi. (our solution)

We chose to work on coccolithophores because they perform the process of calcification to make calcium carbonate shells (coccoliths) using the dissolved carbon dioxide over and above their photosynthetic activity. They are the primary producers of marine CaCO3.

This characteristic makes them suitable candidates to be integrated circularly within areas like the cement industry that require CaCO3 as their raw material and produce huge carbon emissions

Our choice of organism is Emiliania huxleyi, the model organism for coccolithophores.It is the most abundant coccolithophore forming huge blooms which on the global scale release particulate inorganic carbon as calcite in the amounts assessed at 0.4 to 4.8 PgC/yr.

E. huxleyi has the potential to be at the forefront of the war against climate change since it has been shown to exhibit relatively high growth rates and calcification under increased temperatures compared to other coccolithophorids. It is thus able to maintain its growth and photosynthetic efficiency at temperatures that inhibit other species, making it highly resilient to the rising ocean temperatures and changing marine conditions

The process of calcification in E. huxleyi is dependent on several environmental and cellular factors. The CO2 from the atmosphere gets dissolved in the ocean water and forms bicarbonate (HCO3-) ions. These ions are taken in by the algal cell along with Ca+2 present in the surrounding medium. Then through cellular transport they enter a special organelle called the coccolith vessicle (CV) where the reaction for calcium carbonate occurs. The coccolith plates thus formed are taken to the cell boundary where they get deposited in a distinct arrangement providing support and protection to the E. huxelyi cells. Post cellular death, these shells get deposited on the ocean bed and thus sequestering carbon.

Envisioned Implementation & Impact

Our project aims to enhance the process of calcification in E. huxleyi to increase its carbon capture capacity. Through computational modelling, we have identified key cellular and environmental factors that promote calcification. Our in-depth analysis of the algal cell's metabolic pathways led us to select Calmodulin, a major calcium regulator, as our target gene. This research focuses on developing synthetic biology tools tailored for E. huxleyi, optimising its capacity as a natural CO2 sink and integrating it with high-carbon industries such as cement manufacturing.
