Calcification Modelling at Population Level

We wanted to look into how environmental factors impact the overall calcification in the oceanic ecosystem. More importantly, we wanted to get insights into the extent to which the population of Emilania Huxleyi and external nutrient conditions impact the net coccolith production. The population of E. huxleyi is impacted/regulated by a lot of factors including nutrient concentrations, other competing planktons, and various kinds of grazers. Apart from them sunlight (light intensity), temperature and oceanic sinking also plays a role in changing the amount of carbon dioxide sequestered.

So we implemented an Eulerian seasonal phytoplankton model for the purpose of modeling the dynamics of growth of E. huxleyi based upon a few papers as mentioned in the references. The model is one-dimensional with a single layer, the surface mixed layer. Within this layer is a time-dependent ecosystem composed of five classes: E. huxleyi, Diatoms, Dinoflagellates, Phytoflagellates, Picophytoplankton and three classes of heterotrophs: Free Bacteria, Heteroflagellates, Micro Zooplanktons ( such as ciliates ). There are Five Nutrients: Silicates, Nitrate, Phosphate, Ammonium and Dissolved Organic Carbon. Apart from that Detritus Attached Coccoliths and Free Coccoliths have been modeled. The data for the annual variation of light irradiance is passed as input. Temperature is modeled as a sinusoidal function of time.

A typical growth equation looks like this -

dP/dt = [( max_growth_rate * (temperature limitation) * (light limitation) * (combined nutrient limitation)) - (Para1) * (grazer concentration) - (Mortality rate) - (Sinking Rate)]*P

Para1 is a placeholder of a constant/parameter.
P - Population ( in mg Carbon /m3 )
All limitation factors range from 0 to 1.
Grazer can be ciliates or hetero flagellates depending upon the organism under consideration.
Mortality Rate - Incorporates the external mortality rate due to mesograzers and it also incorporates the backflow of carbon due to respiration.

The nutrient limitation and light limitation are modeled using the Michaelis–Menten equation.

Here NH represents the half saturation constant for nitrates. AH represents the same for ammonia. PH similarly for phosphate.

IH represents the half saturation constant for light intensity.
Temperature limitation is modeled a bit differently

A typical nutrient concentration (Nitrogen and Phosphate) equation looks like-

dN/dt = - (Consumption by organisms) + (para2) * (N_baselayer - N)

Consumption by organisms is modeled proportional to carbon intake rate with the coefficient based upon the typical ratio of the nutrient and carbon in the cells of the organism. The second term corresponds to inflow of nutrients from the deeper layer into the mixed layer, the ecosystem of which we are modeling.

Calcification Modelling at a Cellular Level

Introduction

Calcification in E. Huxleyi leads to the formation of coccoliths containing calcite crystals which settle at the bottom of the ocean. Unlike other organic compounds containing carbon which get decomposed and released back to the carbon cycle, deposition of calcite on ocean beds removes it from the carbon cycle and thus plays a significant role as carbons sink. To enhance this process, it is crucial to understand the calcification process within E. Huxleyi and then model the process to understand impacting factors followed by tuning the factors for maximizing calcification.

Calcification pathway in E. Huxleyi

The whole calcification pathway can be majorly divided into 3 steps –

  1. Uptake of calcium and bicarbonate ions from the surrounding environment using channels on the cell membrane and after uptake, free calcium ions being cytotoxic are buffered by cytoplasmic protein calmodulin. Calmodulin, an EF-hand family protein, has a pair of EF-hands with each calmodulin buffering 4 calcium ions at maximal saturation
  2. The actual formation of calcite crystals takes place inside a coccolith vesicle, a specific feature of the coccolithophore family of alga. Inside the vesicle, calcium ions and bicarbonate ions interact to precipitate calcium carbonate, and H+ ions are buffered by the alkaline medium driving the reaction by the Le Chatelier principle. The deposition takes place on an acidic polysaccharides plate forming calcite crystals
  3. These crystals are then carried by some other acidic polysaccharides to the deposition site on the cell wall. The deposition takes place on nucleation sites on the GPA layer which is acidic protein mainly – glutamic acid, proline and alanine.

The uptake, formation and deposition are tightly linked so that the process gets carried smoothly. Before modeling, a detailed literature review was conducted on existing literature to understand the process of calcification and dependencies on various factors. E. huxleyi though a model organism among coccolithophores is relatively unexplored and thus its calcification pathway relatively understudied. Our study and modeling helped us in furthering our knowledge regarding the process.

Initial phase and initial roadblocks

Due to relatively insufficient data, proceeding with modeling which is robust enough to provide conclusive results was difficult. We started looking up for other organisms/ processes undergoing calcification and if there existed models to capture the process to get ideas going ahead with our modeling. We found an interesting paper modeling calcification in bone mineralization.

It was based on differential equations including immature collagen, mature collagen, nucleators (within mature collagen over which calcite deposition takes place), inhibitors (which reduce rate of deposition) and calcification process (which is actual rate of deposition). The immature collagen (over which deposition cannot take place) converts to a mature form of collagen by first order differential equation. The mature form of collagen has nucleator sites over which deposition takes place. These differential equations ensure that during initial phases while collagen is in immature form calcite deposition is low and inhibitor levels are high, as mature collagen forms and inhibitor levels taper, calcification increases drastically. Eventually, nucleator sites also reduce the increase in rate of deposition tapers. Due to this a typical sigmoid-like curve is obtained where rate of growth is slow initially, then increases a lot and then again reduces which is how bone mineralization process takes place spanning 100 days length.

In our case of E huxleyi deposition on GPA protein is not much different. GPA is an acidic protein just like collagen over which nucleation takes place on nucleator sites. Inhibitors can be considered analogous to the feedback process. Unlike immature and mature collagen, GPA is formed in depositional form itself and corresponding changes were made to differential equations. In our case too at the initial 25-30 hours of its 72 hours lifespan, resources are diverted mainly towards growth and calcium deposition rates are low (this trend is followed even at populational level) indicating both structural and functional similarity with bone mineralization process.

Moving Ahead

Modeling a single part of the calcification pathway in isolation wasn’t helpful in terms of understanding how it is contributing to the whole process in general. Thus, modeling all the parts and then connecting all of them as a single end-to-end model was crucial at this point of time. Understanding the coccolith vesicle (CV) which maintained the ambient micro-environment for calcite precipitation was imperative. Coccolith vesicles are of 2 types with pH maintained in them in bimodal distribution – one type having between 6.8 to 7.2 pH and other one between 7.8 to 8.2 pH. Calmodulin unloads its calcium into CV which also uptakes bicarbonate ions and pumps protons actively out of CV into cytoplasm to maintain alkaline conditions. All this pushes the reaction towards formation of calcium carbonate which deposits on acidic polysaccharide plate inside CV on which this precipitation finally forms calcite crystals.

Now according to existing literature, the amount of proteins expressed inside the CV remains the same irrespective of the amount of calcification. When external calcium was reduced to the threshold such that calcification stops, still then CV proteins are expressed to the same extent indicating that CV protein doesn’t act as rate limiting. Rather when external calcium drops, the CV pH is modulated to control the rate of formation of calcite. Acidification of CV is the modality used by E. Huxleyi to stop calcification when external calcium concentration reduces below a threshold. So, the concentration gradient generated because of external calcium concentration and cytoplasmic calcium concentration not only regulates rate of uptake of calcium but also determines the CV pH thereby inhibiting the deposition. In order to capture the idea of CV pH impacting deposition, inhibitor differential equation inculcates dependence with CV pH with low pH leading to lower rate of inhibitor decline thus maintaining higher level of inhibitors for longer times if pH is relatively less alkaline. The CV pH is shown to be on log scale of (external/internal calcium concentration) since pH itself represents log scale with respect to proton ions. pH is adjusted to be in the range of 7 to 8 with respect to external calcium concentration of 0.1 to 10 mM (provided internal calcium concentration is 1e-4 mM maintained by homeostasis to prevent cytotoxicity associated with free calcium ions).

The CV pH is the only factor taken into consideration of the CV in the process since expression and activity of CV otherwise is not limiting and also not dependent on other external factors.

Uptake by calcium channels is dependent on concentration gradient. 10 mM external calcium ions are present in sea water the natural habitat of E. Huxleyi while 0.1 mM is the concentration at which calcification stops. Apart from that, calmodulin which acts as buffering cytoplasmic calcium transporter, also acts as activator for the calcium channels on the cell membrane. Higher amount of free calmodulin leads to higher activation of cell membrane channels leading to higher uptake. After it gets bound to calcium ions, it undergoes conformational change thereby no longer activating channels. This ensures the feedback maintaining the calcium required for calmodulin equilibrium.

Because of the equilibrium constant, the bound calmodulin amount is much higher than free calmodulin. So, the amount of uptake is taken to be dependent on the ratio of external to internal calcium concentration logarithmically (similar to pH) and amount of free calmodulin exponentially (since allosteric activation relates to reduced activation energy). Some amount is used for cellular functions and rest all goes to calcification. This uptake is based on assumption of homeostatic conditions. This correlates with experimental data of 30 to 40 coccoliths being formed at 10 mM external calcium concentration at normally expressed calmodulin amounts.

Since free calcium ions are cytotoxic, internal calcium ion concentration is kept unchanged. All other parameters like coccolith weight, percentage of calcite in coccolith are taken from experimental data. Since total calmodulin is of order 1e-5 , due to equilibrium favoring the bound state to a large extent, the amount of bound state is taken 1e-5 and using the equation of equilibrium constant we get the free state amount to be 1e-7 order. Changing bound state by any factor correlates to similar factor change in free state as well.

Actual model integration

Now all the three parts are combined into a single end-to-end model. We already saw how intricately they are connected. Let the calcium uptake part allocated for calcification be x and the maximal amount which can be deposited by y. In case of x>y, y amount is deposited and x-y is pushed to storage while when y>x, x amount is deposited and uptake acts as limiting in such cases. Here the storage just acts as a placeholder. In actual scenario, there is negative feedback to uptake reducing the uptake in next time stamp but in this case, we are anyway not allowing extra calcium which cannot be deposited to contribute towards our total calcification. Thus, basically we are taking min (x, y) as our contribution towards calcification

Why uptake is limiting than deposition capacity

As can be seen by above graphs only in initial hours, deposition is limited (that too only marginally) and that happens because the cell is yet not ready to divert resources towards calcification and uptake is based on assumption of homeostasis. Later on, for the majority of time intervals, uptake is limited. It can be easily seen from the graphs that increasing uptake will improve rate of calcification and cytotoxicity of free calcium ions will force the cell to accelerate its coccolith production. Deposition capability doesn’t act as RDS in this pathway.

Why calmodulin over simply increasing external calcium concentration

Increasing external calcium concentration by 10 % over 10 mM of normal external medium leads to 2.12 % increase in total calcification over the lifespan whereas 10% increase in calmodulin expression leads to 10.79% increase in total calcification. Also, though external calcium concentration increment is easier than increased expression of calmodulin, it would stop having effect in actual practice beyond a limit due to saturation of channels and negative feedback. Thus, enhanced calmodulin expression leading to enhanced calcium uptake can be considered as a way for improving calcification rate drastically.

SRA Gene Expression Study

Abstract

Emiliania huxleyi (E. huxleyi) is a cosmopolitan coccolithophore (self-feeding single-celled phytoplankton) that plays a crucial role in the global carbon cycle. It performs photosynthetic carbon fixation and calcification to synthesize its calcified scales. Understanding the genetic mechanisms behind carbon fixation and calcification, which can serve as an understanding of the internal calcification process of E huxleyi, is challenging due to a lack of quantitative data. The biochemical steps and metabolic mechanisms underlying calcification remain poorly defined, limiting our ability to characterize this critical process fully. Here, we leveraged publicly available transcriptomics (RNA-seq) data to decipher a likely calcification pathway and the underlying mechanisms of its regulation. More importantly, we also develop an analytical framework and tool called genecorder, that enables rapid analysis of public RNA-seq datasets and identification of genes associated with a particular phenotype.

Background

E. huxleyi is a haptophyte (Figure 1) that produces delicate calcium carbonate scales called cocoliths by fixing the carbon dioxide from their environment. The E. huxleyi genome was first constructed and studied in a landmark paper that provided a foundational resource for further transcriptomic and genomic studies aimed at understanding its gene functions. The genome, sequenced from strain CCMP1516, was complemented by sequences from 13 additional isolates, revealing a pan-genome structure, with core genes shared among strains and others variably distributed. This work has paved the way for deeper insights into how calcification-related genes may be regulated across different strains of E. huxleyi (Read et al., 2013). The reference genome and associated files were first made publicly available on the JGI Phycocosm database (Download - Emiliania huxleyi CCMP1516 v1.0 (doe.gov)). Building upon this reference, Ensembl has made available an updated genome and transcript annotation file (Details - Emiliania_huxleyi - Ensembl Genomes 59). The current assembly (v1.0) of E. huxelyi consists of a total of 7795 scaffolds and 38, 788 unique genes.

While CCMP1516 and CCMP371 are ‘calcifying strains’ of E. huxleyi, other strains such as CCMP2090 have lost the ability to calcify (http://dx.doi.org/10.1093/pcp/pcs066). Nam et al. (https://www.e-algae.org/journal/view.php?doi=10.4490/algae.2018.33.4.21) performed a transcriptomic study to compare a calcifying strain, CCMP371 with a non-calcifying strain, CCMP2090. Nam et al. identified several genes associated with coccolith (calcium scales of algae) production that were differentially expressed across the strains. By modulating the calcium concentration in growth media, Nam et al. further characterized that a minimum concentration of 10mM calcium is required for E. huxleyi to sustain its calcifying abilities and identified genes whole expression pattern varied as a function of calcium concentration. For example, eIF4A (ID 312754), profilin (ID 364205), ATP-binding cassette transporter (ID 231423) were the few top differentially expressed as a function of calcium concentration (Figure 2). Most of the top differentially expressed genes are either membrane-bound or transporter genes thereby making them difficult to study experimentally as they usually lack flexibility and stability.

Nam et al. used CCMP1516 which is a calcifying strain of E. huxleyi for their study. While their study characterised which genes are associated with eliciting fixation response at different calcium concentration. Their study characterized genes associated with the calcium synthesis pathway in the calcifying strain. However some strains, such as CCMP2090, are non-calcifying thereby providing an opportunity to characterize genes that are causal for calcification. Viral infections have been shown to have a slower effect on calcifying strains like CCMP1516 compared to non-calcifying strains, further emphasizing the unique biological properties of calcification (Hanusot et al. 2020).

Richier et al., 2009, examined the expression of genes of biogeochemical interest in calcifying (diploid) and noncalcifying (haploid) strains of E.huxleyi. One of the genes that Richier et al. identified as differentially expressed is calmodulin (CAM), which shows a huge contrast in expression between diploid and haploid stages as compared to other proteins. CAM plays a crucial role in regulating calcium uptake, transport, and secretion. Given the infeasibility of experimentally examining every upregulated gene, we want to filter our genes of interest by excluding those encoding membrane-bound proteins, as they pose challenges in experimental validation.

Given CAM is a cytosolic protein, we hypothesized it to be the most appropriate target gene whose gene expression could be modulated in E. huxleyi to enhance carbon dioxide fixation, i.e. enhance calfiication. Unlike membrane-bound proteins, which are difficult to study experimentally, CAM's structural flexibility and soluble nature make it more amenable to biochemical and molecular research. By focusing on E. huxleyi CAM, we aim to investigate its specific role in calcification, whether it is involved directly or modulated by other factors in the calcium homeostasis pathway.

Next, we performed a thorough characterization of the sequence and structural properties of CAM1 in E huxelyi. First, we performed a BLAST analysis of E. huxel, we discovered a 90% sequence similarity between E. huxleyi CAM and human CAM. Human CAM is extensively studied for its involvement in various cellular processes, including cell differentiation, polarization, and nucleation. E. huxleyi CAM1 sequence has 150 amino acids. While the structural properties of Human CAM1 are very well characterized, we used AlphaFoldv3( https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-024-01385-x) to predict the structure of CAM1 in E.huxelyi (Figure 4). Similar to the human ortholog, CAM1 in E. huxelyi has two approximately symmetrical globular domains (corresponding to the N- and C- domains) which are separated by a flexible linker region where Ca2+ binds.

Given that calcification is a complex process involving multiple pathways and genes, it’s important to differentiate between CAM’s role in general calcium homeostasis and its specific involvement in calcification. In the sections that follow, we tease apart the actual role of CAM (CAM1) using public single-cell RNA-sequencing datasets.

Deciphering the gene regulatory network of CAM1

We hypothesized that the lack of CAM1 expression in the non-calcifying strain CCMP2090 could be explained by the difference in the gene regulatory network associated with CAM1. Since currently there are no ChIP-seq or ATAC-seq datasets available for either of these strains, we used a simple correlation-based approach to identify which transcription factors are associated with CAM1.

We identified a set of transcription factors (Figure 4) that have a high correlation with CAM1 in CCMP1516 but a low correlation in the CCMP2090 sample. While we could not perform an in-vivo or in-vitro validation of these transcription factors, this list can be used by future studies to characterize the each molecular mechanism behind CAM1’s regulation in E.huxleyi.

Discussion and Conclusion

The box plot demonstrates a clear variation in the expression levels of the CAM1 gene across different calcium concentrations, implying that calcium availability may influence CAM1 transcription. Notably, the increased expression at 10 mM calcium compared to lower concentrations suggests a potential role for calcium in regulating the gene's activity, possibly as part of a broader calcium signaling pathway. The higher median expression at elevated calcium levels could indicate that CAM1 plays a role in processes that are upregulated in calcium-rich environments, such as calcification or calcium homeostasis. These findings may have broader implications for understanding how Emiliania huxleyi modulates gene expression in response to environmental calcium, which could be crucial for its calcification process and its ecological success as a coccolithophore. Further studies could explore whether CAM1 is directly regulated by calcium or if its expression changes are a downstream effect of broader metabolic shifts.

The box plot highlights a clear difference in CAM1 gene expression between the Emiliania huxleyi strains CCMP1516 and CCMP2090, with strain CCMP1516 exhibiting significantly higher expression levels. The elevated CAM1 expression in CCMP1516 could imply that this strain has enhanced mechanisms related to carbon fixation or calcium-related metabolic processes, providing a possible explanation for its distinct physiological traits. In the upcoming analysis, we further study this.

In contrast, the lower CAM1 expression in CCMP2090 may reflect differences in its metabolic pathways or ecological adaptations. These findings have important implications for understanding strain-specific differences in E. huxleyi, particularly in how these strains respond to environmental factors like carbon availability and may offer insights into their distinct roles in global carbon cycling and their ecological success in different habitats.

The contrasting correlation patterns between the two strains may also point to broader transcriptional network integration. In CCMP1516, CAM1 and its associated TFs could be part of a highly co-regulated network, where multiple TFs work in concert to control a set of genes involved in related metabolic processes. In CCMP2090, this co-regulation might be less pronounced, with the transcription factors regulating different sets of genes or being involved in more diverse pathways.

  • In CCMP1516: The transcription factors may act synergistically to control CAM1, creating a tightly interconnected network that ensures coordinated gene expression. This could be crucial for maintaining efficient carbon fixation and calcification.
  • In CCMP2090:The regulatory network could be more modular or loosely integrated, with each transcription factor controlling distinct sets of genes. As a result, CAM1 is not as tightly co-regulated with the transcription factors, leading to weaker correlations.
