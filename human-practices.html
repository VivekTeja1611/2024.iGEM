Integrated Human Practices

Survey

In our iGEM Team’s Integrated Human Practices, we prioritize a collaborative and reflective approach. Our project is built on the belief that sustainable solutions must be shaped by a wide range of perspectives, which is why we actively sought input from industry experts and stakeholders across various fields concerned with our project. We conducted a comprehensive survey involving participants from various age groups, aiming to gather a broad understanding of public perceptions regarding climate change and environmental solutions. The survey revealed that a majority of respondents acknowledged the significant impact of the climate crisis on both the environment and society. In terms of action priorities, the survey results emphasized reducing fossil fuel consumption, increasing investment in renewable energy and protecting forests. Interestingly, when it came to the implementation of genetic engineering for environmental solutions, there was clear skepticism along with a notable lack of awareness around technologies such as carbon capture and storage, as well as the specific role of coccolithophores in the global carbon cycle.

Undestanding the Problem

Meeting with climate activist Ridhima Pandey

Description of person: Ridhima is a young environmental activist who has advocated for action against climate change. She was also part of the 2019 UN Climate Action Summit.
Reason for approach: Our main reason for interviewing her was to realise the challenges we could face in our project as a climate crisis solution, learn from her journey, and what has kept her motivated through the challenges that have come her way. We also discussed the major climate issues faced in India and the existing solutions, such as renewable energy sources and zero waste policies, for this.
Inputs received: On the question of why India needs to improve in tackling these issues, she said that people generally do not take it seriously. The lack of awareness and ignorance just adds fuel to the fire.. Raising our voices and encouraging and asking the decision-makers to take action is essential in this fight. Her major point was to be the change you want to see in the world. These words hold great significance for the younger generation, who will grow up in a time when climate issues are causing severe harm to the world. Taking action now will make the world a better place for them.
Integration in the project: Her words were an inspiration to us and have thus motivated us to conduct many such informative sessions regarding the climate crisis and approaches such as synthetic biology targeted towards school and college students, involving relevant professors and organisations as well.

Advice from Academicians

Meeting with Prof. Reshamwala

Description of person: Dr. Shamlan M. S. Reshamwala is a professor at the Institute of Chemical Technology, Mumbai with expertise in recombinant proteins, enzyme engineering and bioprospecting to explore metabolic diversity.
Reason for approach: We approached him to understand the challenges that may be involved in working with a novel organism like E. huxleyi and how to go about this process in a systematic manner.
Inputs received: In our meeting with the professor, several challenges and strategic directions for our project were discussed. One of the key insights was that deploying our solution in marine environments presents challenges, which limits us to coastal regions. To move forward, we will need to acquire the E. huxleyi strain and obtain the necessary research tools under a research license, which will allow us to explore the organism in depth. We were also told by the professor that Plasmids are hard to acquire. He also suggested using a directed evolution approach to acclimatize the E. huxleyi strain to higher temperatures by gradually increasing it by 1°C, ensuring adaptability to varying conditions. For media engineering, a design of experiments approach was recommended, involving multiple runs to identify critical parameters. A detailed CO2 absorption study was proposed, beginning with a control test in an alkaline medium, followed by experiments with the algae to measure the CO2 difference through titration. Furthermore, the importance of reviewing publications on the genetic engineering of E. huxleyi and obtaining plasmids from sources like AddGene was highlighted. Another potential avenue is creating a synthetic organelle for calcification, with examples like diol toxins in Salmonella. However, a realistic approach is essential, and overly undefined systems, like attempting to create synthetic coccolith vesicles, may not be practical as we do not have enough information about it. Thus this meeting gave us a very good overview of the complexities we might encounter in our project and how we could navigate around them using innovative implementation. Post this meeting we decided to discuss the implementation of his inputs with our Prof. Balaji from our college.

Meeting with Prof. PV Balaji


Description: Dr. PV Balaji is the current head of the Department of Biosciences and Bioengineering at IIT Bombay. His research is focused on computational biology and bioinformatics.
Reason for approach: We approached him to discuss the insights we had gained from some of our past meetings with Academicians. We also wanted to get his guidance on how to implement some of the key ideas we had learnt uptill now.
Inputs received: The professor advised us that working with overly undefined systems like the metabolism of E. huxleyi is a very tricky task. He said that although the idea of creating a synthetic organelle is novel and exciting, it is important for us as a team to be realistic in our ideation and do feasibility checks. He iterated on the importance of studying the functioning of CaM since it would determine the main step of an understudied system. This would help us make a foundational advance and create avenues for collaboration with future iGEM teams. This meeting helped us understand the complexities involved in modifications of biological systems and how we must take a grounded approach while designing our solution. We also increased our focus on studying CaM and figuring out its role in the calcium pathway of our algae.

Meeting with Prof. Amal Kanti Bera

Description of person: Dr. Bera is a professor in the Department of Biotechnology at the Indian Institute of Technology, Madras (IITM). The professor’s expertise is in the area of ion channels and their structure- function relationships.
Reason for approach: We approached him to gain insights about working on cellular processes involving ion transport and signaling especially with respect to the Calcium ions involved in the Calcification process.
Inputs received: He gave insights about how to think about the calcification enhancement through activation of specific Ca+2 receptors. We learnt about the concept of ‘optogenetics’, where specific wavelengths of light can be used to open certain ion channels like channel Rhodopsin- to effectively increase the intracellular calcium concentration. This would allow us to have enhanced control over the modified cells as compared to direct overexpression of a protein.
Integration in the project: After getting his inputs, our team realized the importance of ion channels in the calcification process of E. huxleyi like the calcium pumps in the coccolith vesicle and the Ca channels on the cell membrane. His emphasis on the binding of proteins and role of receptors also helped us to choose Calmodulin (CaM) as our gene of interest because of the role in plays in the calcium signaling pathway. This also motivated us to use computational modeling to get a better understanding of the Ca+2 receptors and pumps.

Meeting with Prof. Rajesh Patkar


Description: Prof. Rajesh Patkar from the Biosciences and Bioengineering Department at IITB, has significant experience in molecular biology and biotechnology.
Reason for approach: We wanted to understand how culturing of algae is done on lab scale as well as industrial scale so we approached Prof Rajesh Patkar who has expertise in plant and fungal culturing in our institute.
Inputs received: He helped us understand the importance of various environmental factors and nutrient mediums while growing a species. He emphasized on how even minute changes in these can significantly affect the growth rates of organisms. Thus these insights helped us to begin our work on optimization of the nutrient conditions for E. huxelyi cultures through modeling.

National Centre for Antarctic and Ocean Research, Goa

Description: Dr. Shramik Patil and Dr. Rahul Mohan are researchers at the National Centre for Antarctic and Ocean Research, studying marine micro and nanoplanktons and coccolithophores, a very relevant area for our problem statement.
Reason for approach: Through this discussion, our main aim was to gain insights into culturing E. huxleyi on a laboratory as well as industrial scale, and understanding how to determine the conditions for it. We also wanted to learn about techniques to quantify the calcification. To gain insights into how to culture E. huxleyi, we discussed with Dr. Shramik about his work on RCC1355 and other coccolithophores, telling about what conditions were used by him for his experiment. Information on the strain and the environment where it has been taken from is necessary to decide the conditions for culturing.
Inputs received: They shared papers with us that had data about coccolith mass and size, correlating it with the CaCO3 content. They also talked about a study of their own involving coccolith mass variation with factors such as pH, irradiance and temperature.
Integration in the project: Their work on E. huxleyi and knowledge about it in terms of its morphology and in context of its growth in the local natural environment helped us in our population modelling. We were able to incorporate a calcification process in the organism while also considering the growth impacting environmental factors.


Meeting with K.V.Venkatesh

Description of person: Dr. K. V. Venkatesh is a professor at IIT Bombay, with his research covering the areas of Systems Biology, Network analysis and modeling, Synthetic Biology and Metabolic and regulatory networks research. He has also served as an international judge for iGEM in the past.
Reason for approach: To gain a better understanding of the competition in terms of what areas we should focus on and how we should direct our work, we decided to meet him and get the perspective of someone who has been on the other side of the judging panel.
Inputs received: He gave us insights regarding judging in iGEM, the main aspects of a project judges usually focus on, etc. These key insights were very useful for a new team like ours. Along with that, he suggested that we focus more on the biobricks aspect for our offered solution, highlighting the importance of contribution to the overall iGEM community.

Insights from Industry

Meeting with Agromorph

Description: Akansha Agarwal is the founder of Agromorph, a tech company which focuses on algae-based products and technologies within India.
Reason for approach: We decided to contact them since they had industrial experience in developing algal based products and could give insights on how we could possibly upscale it from the lab environment.
Inputs received: It was advised to us that we should specifically point out the geographical locations where our strain of marine algae is obtained from and functions. This would be beneficial as we will be able to recognise which conditions are to be used for the algae. With regards to upscaling, we were asked to keep a number of backup systems and cultures. This would be useful in the case of a culture collapsing due to unexpected conditions as there would be other cultures which would still be capturing carbon. While scaling up, we would need to make sure that we replicate every small component of the lab scale.
Integration in the project: The meet was very insightful for us and helped us to make our solution more detailed while also educating us about some basic aspects of upscaling to industrial processes. Meeting with Sea6 Energy

Description of person: Sri Sailaja Nori is the Co-founder and Director of Research and Development of Sea6 Energy, a seaweed company that focuses on cultivating and processing tropical seaweed species.
Reason for approach: We had a meet with her as she had knowledge about cultivation of seaweed and algae while also possessing knowledge about the iGEM competition.
Inputs received: During our meeting with Sri Sailaja Nori from Sea6 Energy, we received valuable feedback on how to position our iGEM project as a foundational advance in synthetic biology. We were advised to present the biobrick we are designing as a tool that future teams can build upon, emphasizing its wide range of potential applications across various industries. By doing so, we contribute meaningfully to the iGEM community by creating a resource that can be adapted for various synthetic biology challenges. Additionally, we were told about why not to use bioreactors or artificial ponds for microalgae culturing, as this would necessitate lab-scale proof of concept and specific numerical data, which we are not yet equipped to provide.
Integration in the project: This strategic direction ensured that our project remains focused on contributing foundational tools for future iGEM teams rather than specific applications that may be not feasible technically at this stage.


Meeting with Mr. Jay Prakash Tiwari

Description of person: Jay is an industry professional who is working as the Head of Technology and Decarbonization in Jindal Steel & Power Ltd.
Reason for approach: We approached him as he had experience in the carbon capture methods utilised in the steel industry and has put efforts in making innovative technology for the same.
Inputs received: He emphasized the challenge of converting CO2 into useful chemicals due to the high enthalpy required, advocating for the use of green hydrogen as an energy source. He shared details of pilot projects utilizing physical and chemical adsorption methods for CO2 capture, such as the Cold Methanol and Methyl Diamine processes. A notable point was the potential of algae-based carbon capture, despite its high resource demands. He stressed the importance of improving the efficiency of biological pathways, such as the calcification process in algae, and the necessity of looping calcium for sustainability.
Integration in the project: His inputs helped us look into possible industries in which we could integrate our solution, thus beginning our analysis of the Cement Industry which needs Calcium Carbonate as its raw material and generates a significant carbon footprint during its operation. This interview also highlighted the need for precise thermodynamic and kinetic calculations to optimize carbon capture processes and enhance their scalability.
On similar lines, we also received inputs from several professors including Prof. Santosh Narohna from the chemical engineering department at IITB, about how we will need to go about performing material balances and thermodynamic analysis as chemical engineers to come up with estimates for the capex and opex for our carbon capture solution, when we are ready to integrate it with the industry.

Discussions with Mr Gyanendra Dixit at UltraTech

Description: Mr. Gyanendra Dixit is a process engineer working with UltraTech Cement.
Reason for approach: We decided to approach him to see if our proposed implementation plan of integrating our algal system with industries like cement was feasible.

Visit to UltraTech Cement Facility

To gain a better understanding of the concepts that we had discussed above, we arranged a visit to an Ultratech Cement facility.

During the onsite visit, our team got to understand the detailed overview of the cement manufacturing process and the quality control involved in the same. We learnt about the two kinds of cement OPC and PPC. Our main question for them was to know what all quality parameters will need to be met in order to integrate our biobased calcium carbonate as the raw material for the clinker process. This led us to gain in depth insights about how the industry has strict requirements to meet the official standards set by the government. They strongly advised us to look into the current standards and get a perspective on the kind of raw material the industry is mandated to use for its operation. Based on this input, our team studied the website of Bureau of Indian Standards (Click here) and found the IS 18189 : 2023 standard which specifies the raw materials for the cement industry :

Thus we decided to look closely into the coccolith structures and characteristics of the CaCO3 we will be producing so that it can be potentially integrated as a viable technological solution.
These interactions helped us to gain the perspective of the industrial stakeholders and their requirements.

iGEM Community

Interaction with Riya Madan, student in the IISER Mohali iGEM team

Description of person: She had valuable expertise as the Co-Head of the Dry Lab team of the IISER Mohali iGEM team.
Reason for approach: While attempting to model calcification in our algae, there were several major challenges that we were getting stuck at. So we decided to discuss how to deal with the complexities of modeling biological systems with Riya.
Inputs received: This meeting was an important step where the team was able to understand the correct way to approach this complex modeling problem. We learnt that it is crucial to break down the problem into parts and compartmentalize the system based on the functions. We can then compare these to analogous processes in other organisms. For example developing the calcium deposition pathway based on the bone mineralization process in humans and calcification in oysters. We also learnt how to deal with the data scarcity issues of E. huxleyi by looking at relative changes using approximations instead of scarce absolute data. This meeting was crucial in helping us gain a correct starting point to model our complex process and helped us understand how to focus on individual components of the biological system and integrate them based on their dependencies to predict our desired output.
