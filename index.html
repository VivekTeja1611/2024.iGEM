Our Earth
is suffering

According to the United Nations' Net Zero Goals, limiting global warming to no more than 1.5°C requires a 45% reduction in emissions by 2030 and achieving net zero by 2050. This problem is at the center of the UN Sustainable Development Goal of Climate Action.

But...

Current solutions of Carbon Capture and Storage fail to match the requirements and come with huge concerns about long-term impact. The International Energy Agency (IEA) also observed in its 2023 net zero roadmap that ‘removing carbon from the atmosphere is costly and uncertain.’

To tackle this pressing issue, we took inspiration from a marine coccolithophore algae called Emiliania huxleyi.
Coccolithophores are a type of green marine algae that produce calcium carbonate shells called coccoliths.

These shells form a long term carbon sink due to their inorganic nature. It is estimated that the coccolithophorid algae represent up to half of all existing global CaCO3 production.

Can we harness this calcification process to develop a solution for carbon capture?
